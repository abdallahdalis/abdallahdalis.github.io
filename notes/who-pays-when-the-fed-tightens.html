Who pays when the Fed tightens?

Abdallah Dalis · July 2026 · 4 min read

I shipped the first paper of my research program to SSRN last week: "Rate Sensitivity and Employment: A Shift-Share Difference-in-Differences of Federal Funds Rate Changes on County-Level Employment."

The question behind the whole program is simple to say and hard to answer: when the Fed raises rates, who actually pays? National averages hide the answer. A rate hike is one number in Washington, but its cost lands unevenly across thousands of local labor markets.

Part 1 measures that unevenness directly. I build a panel of U.S. counties from 2002 to 2019 and compare employment in counties more exposed to rate changes against counties less exposed, before and after Fed moves. The headline result is a puzzle: more exposed counties do not lose jobs after tightening, and the raw estimate even leans slightly positive. That contradicts the textbook story and older regional evidence like Carlino and DeFina.

The positive sign should not be read as causal, and the paper says so plainly. Exposure to rate changes is not randomly assigned, and the Fed raises rates when the economy is strong, so strong labor markets and tightening arrive together. There is also a pre-trend at one of the placebo lags, which I disclose rather than hide. The claim the paper can support is about differential exposure, not a clean monetary shock.

That is what Part 2 is for. Using high-frequency surprises around FOMC announcements (the Bauer-Swanson series) and local projections, the expected contractionary effect comes back. The puzzle in Part 1 is an identification artifact, and showing that step by step is the point of publishing both parts as a sequence.

Part 3 asks the distributional question: once the contraction is identified cleanly, is the employment cost larger in higher-minority counties? The early answer is that the gradient exists between metro areas rather than within them, though the estimates are still imprecise. That one is in progress.

I sat on Part 1 for two months waiting to make it perfect. Shipping it with the limitation disclosed was the better move: the flaw is scoped, the finding is honest, and the program only reads as a program if the parts actually come out.

The paper is on SSRN, and the code and data construction are on GitHub. If you work on monetary policy, regional labor markets, or place-based incidence, I would love to hear from you.
